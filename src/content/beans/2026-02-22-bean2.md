### Context

Second cup, first brew of the Guatemala.
Beans were stored in the freezer prior to grinding.
Using purified water.

While pouring the grounds into the preheated filter cone, I paused to smell
them. The heat rising from the cone carried the aroma upward into my face,
more immediate, almost amplified. My 🦄 noted it smelled darker than the first
coffee this morning. That feels right.

There is something about pouring all 308 g of preheat water into the Switch,
leaving the kettle empty, that makes the water feel singular and deliberate.
Nothing split between vessels. One body of water, fully accounted for.

### Coffee

- **Roaster:** Parable Coffee Co.
- **Coffee name:** Guatemala
- **Blend component:** 0.8 g Parable Coffee Co. Mocha Java (accidental inclusion)
- **Total dose (intended):** 16 g
- **Actual grounds:** 15.7 g
- **Water (brew):** 255.6 g
- **Water (preheat):** 308 g
- **Bag notes:** (not referenced yet)
- **Roast date:** (not recorded)

Grounds visually contain small white flecks, something I have seen before in
Parable coffees, including Ruby Colorful Coffees. Not universal across coffees.
Possibly chaff fragments or lighter internal bean material exposed by fracture.

### Gear

- **Brewer:** Hario Switch
- **Grinder:** Timemore C5 ESP Pro (1.8.3)
- **Filter:** paper
- **Water:** purified

### Plan

- **Recipe:** RTT (three spritzes)
- **Dose target:** 16 g (undershot to 15.7 g)
- **Water target:** 256 g (actual 255.6 g)
- **Grind:** 1.8.3 (baseline hold)
- **Preheat:** Easy immersion, full-volume pour into Switch

Holding grind constant to establish first-expression baseline. Not adjusting
yet for darker roast appearance.

### Technique notes

Preheated via easy immersion, then poured entire preheat volume into the
Switch, emptying the kettle completely.

RTT with three spritzes.

Smelling the grounds in a hot cone is a notable ritual moment. Heat volatilizes
aromatics differently than smelling from the catch cup.

### Brew notes (timed)

Bloom expansion moderate.
Surface held tension without aggressive bubbling.
Drawdown steady.

Spent bed resembles earlier cup:
A faint high-water mark along the perimeter. Grounds appear to have slid
downward during drawdown, forming a central mound. Slight wall adhesion, then
interior clearing.

![Post-brew bed showing central mound and perimeter high-water mark](/beans/images/2026-02-22-sun-bed-2.png)

No obvious channel holes. More "wash toward center" than flat immersion bed.

### Observations

**Dry aroma (heated cone):** darker roast profile, cocoa, faint toasted sugar,
less fruit-forward than earlier coffee.

**Wet aroma:** round, deeper, slightly earthy.

**First sip:** softer acidity; darker entry.
**Mid-palate:** cocoa forward, muted fruit.
**Acidity:** low, subdued; mostly mid-palate.
**Sweetness:** present but restrained; brown sugar rather than bright fruit.
**Bitterness:** gentle, integrated; not sharp.
**Body:** medium; slightly fuller than first cup.
**Aftertaste:** short cocoa finish.

The 0.8 g Mocha Java inclusion is negligible proportionally (~5%), but it may
subtly deepen chocolate notes.

### Reflection

The darker aroma aligns with both visual cues and cup structure. The persistent
"slide to center" bed pattern across two coffees suggests something systematic
in drawdown flow rather than bean-specific behavior. Undershooting dose by
0.3 g is likely minimal in impact, but worth noting as part of a developing
scratchpad discipline.

Going forward, maintaining a living "Current Recipe" document, including
preheat protocol, pour consolidation, grind baseline, and agitation philosophy,
could help separate technique evolution from bean expression. This cup feels
like the beginning of that codification.
